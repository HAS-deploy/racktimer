RackTimer vs Strong: as a rest timer

RackTimer vs Strong: Which Is the Better Rest Timer?

Both apps can time your rest periods between sets. But they were built with different goals in mind, and that difference shows up the moment you start using them.

What Each App Is

RackTimer is purpose-built for one job: timing rest periods. No workout logging, no exercise library, no account required. You open it, set your rest duration, and it counts down. That's the full pitch.

Strong is a full-featured workout tracker. It logs exercises, tracks volume over time, graphs your progress, and — among many other things — includes a built-in rest timer that fires automatically after each logged set.

This matters because you're not really comparing two rest timers. You're comparing a dedicated tool against a feature inside a much larger tool.

Head-to-Head: Rest Timer Features

| Feature | RackTimer | Strong | |---|---|---| | Dedicated rest timer | Yes | Yes (within workout log) | | Requires account / login | No | No (free tier exists) | | Ads | No | No (free tier has upsells) |

| Feature | RackTimer | Strong | |---|---|---| | Auto-starts after logged set | No | Yes | | Custom rest durations per exercise | No | Yes (Pro) | | Audio + haptic alerts | Yes | Yes |

Where RackTimer Wins

Speed and Simplicity

There is no friction. You don't log a set, confirm a rep count, or navigate a menu. You tap, it runs. For lifters who already know their programming and just need a countdown, this is genuinely faster to use mid-session.

No Account, No Data, No Nonsense

RackTimer doesn't ask for an email address, doesn't sync to a cloud, and doesn't nudge you toward a subscription. If privacy or minimalism matters to you, this is a meaningful advantage.

Cognitive Load at the Rack

When you're between a heavy squat set and your next attempt, a single-purpose screen showing a big countdown number is easier to process than a full workout log. RackTimer's interface is optimized for a fatigued brain.

No Ads

Strong's free tier surfaces upsells toward its Pro subscription. They aren't obnoxious, but they exist. RackTimer has none.

Where Strong Wins

Automatic Timer Triggering

This is Strong's biggest practical advantage as a rest timer. Once you log a set, the countdown starts on its own. You don't have to remember to tap anything — your hands might still be on the bar. For lifters who use Strong for logging anyway, this is a significant quality-of-life feature.

Per-Exercise Rest Targets

Strong Pro lets you assign different default rest periods to different exercises. Your bench press rests for 3 minutes; your cable curls rest for 60 seconds. RackTimer requires you to manually change the duration when you switch movements. If you train with varied rest periods across a session, Strong handles this more elegantly.

Integration With Your Workout Data

Because Strong logs everything, the rest timer exists alongside your sets, reps, and weights. You can glance at your previous sets while you're resting. That context is genuinely useful and something a standalone timer cannot offer.

Progression Tracking

This isn't a rest-timer feature, but it's worth naming: if you're not already tracking workouts somewhere else, Strong gives you a reason to consolidate. A rest timer that also remembers your PRs is more useful than one that doesn't.

Honest Limitations of Each

RackTimer's limitations are real. It doesn't auto-start, doesn't remember per-exercise preferences, and offers nothing beyond the countdown itself. If you find yourself manually resetting it constantly because your rest times vary, that friction adds up. It also won't tell you anything about your workout — it's a stopwatch with a UI, not a training tool.

Strong's rest timer has its own rough edges. If you use Strong for logging but occasionally forget to log a set, the timer doesn't start. The timer is dependent on the logging workflow — break the workflow, lose the automation. Strong's free tier also has limits on how many exercises or routines you can create, which can push people toward Pro ($9.99/month or ~$29.99/year at the time of writing, though pricing may have changed).

Who Actually Needs What

Use RackTimer if:

  • You already track workouts in a notebook, spreadsheet, or another app
  • You want something open instantly without navigating a logging interface
  • You follow a simple program with consistent rest periods
  • You dislike accounts, subscriptions, and data collection
  • You want the lowest-friction possible option between sets

Use Strong if:

  • You want your rest timer to start automatically after each set
  • You train with multiple exercises that have different rest durations
  • You aren't already logging workouts somewhere else
  • The per-exercise rest target feature (Pro) would save you mental energy
  • You want progress tracking alongside timing

Use both if:

Some lifters log in Strong but find its timer interface a half-second slower to read mid-set. Using RackTimer as a secondary screen while logging in Strong is an unusual choice, but it's a legitimate one if you find big-number countdowns easier to glance at.

The Honest Verdict

For pure rest timing with zero setup, RackTimer is the better tool. It does one thing without getting in the way.

For lifters who log workouts in Strong anyway, the built-in timer is probably good enough — and the auto-start feature genuinely outperforms anything a standalone timer can offer in terms of convenience.

Pick RackTimer if you want a frictionless, no-account timer and you're tracking your training elsewhere.

Pick Strong if you want your rest timer to be part of a complete logging system, especially if auto-start and per-exercise durations matter to your workflow.

Neither is objectively better. It depends on what's already in your pocket when you walk into the gym.
